# Coverage affects the accuracy of modification detection

To investigate the effect of sequencing coverage on detection accuracy, we subsampled native and IVT rRNA sequencing data to 25 coverage levels ranging from 5x to 1000x using Filtlong (Wick, 2017/2022), which prioritises longer reads (>=500 bp) to maintain coverage across the full transcript length. We then evaluated the performance of nine tools (DiffErr was excluded as it failed to produce valid output at any coverage level) in identifying known RNA modifications on E. coli 16S and 23S rRNA.

## Tool output completeness

Five tools, JACUSA2, ELIGOS, Tombo, xPore, and EpiNano, produced valid output across all 25 coverage levels (Figure X). DRUMMER required a minimum of 15x (23S) or 25x (16S) coverage, while Yanocomp required >=20x (23S) or >=35x (16S). Nanocompore produced output files at most coverages, but often failed AUROC/AUPRC validity because >50% of reported scores were invalid in the middle coverage range. For 16S, valid metrics were obtained for 3/3 replicates at 5x and 10x, 1/3 replicates at 15x, none at 20-65x, and then resumed from 70x onward (all 3 replicates valid by 80x). For 23S, valid metrics were obtained for 1 replicate at 5x and 1 of 2 available replicates at 10x, none at 15-50x, and then resumed from 55x onward. At 5x, one 23S Nanocompore replicate was filtered out during Nanocompore reference coverage filtering, producing an empty result database. nanoDoc was evaluated at 15 predefined coverage levels (5x, 10x, 20x, 30x, 40x, 50x, 60x, 70x, 80x, 90x, 100x, 150x, 200x, 500x, and 1000x).

## AUROC vs coverage

To enable fair comparison, we report universal AUROC values computed over all reference positions, with missing predictions assigned a default score of zero (see Methods). AUROC increased with coverage for all tools, with performance generally stabilising at 100-200x (Figure 1). JACUSA2 achieved the highest AUROC across both references, reaching 0.96 (23S) and 0.93 (16S) at 1000x coverage, and was the first tool to surpass 0.9, doing so at >=70x for 23S rRNA and >=30x for 16S. ELIGOS performed similarly, exceeding 0.9 at >=45x (23S) and >=75x (16S). Tombo reached 0.91 (23S) at 1000x but required >=500x coverage to exceed 0.9. Yanocomp showed a distinctive late-onset trajectory, rising from near-random AUROC (0.50) at its minimum functional coverage of 20x to 0.91 at 1000x, coinciding with a decline in its no-call rate from ~100% to 32%. DRUMMER, Nanocompore, EpiNano, and xPore achieved moderate to low universal AUROC (0.57-0.75 at 1000x), though for DRUMMER and xPore this reflected high no-call rates (94.5% and 71.3%, respectively) rather than poor intrinsic discrimination; their reported AUROC values, computed only over positions where predictions were made, were 0.98 and 0.92, respectively.

## AUPRC vs coverage

Given the severe class imbalance in our dataset (modification prevalence 25/3304 = 0.00757 [0.76%] for 23S; 11/1942 = 0.00566 [0.57%] for 16S), we used AUPRC to assess the trade-off between precision and recall (Figure 2). The expected AUPRC for a random classifier equals the class prevalence (0.00757 for 23S and 0.00566 for 16S). AUPRC revealed a more differentiated performance landscape than AUROC. At 1000x on 23S rRNA, JACUSA2 achieved the highest AUPRC (0.52, ~69x above random), followed by DRUMMER (0.44, ~58x), ELIGOS (0.27, ~35x), Yanocomp (0.13), Tombo (0.11), and the remaining tools (0.04-0.08). JACUSA2 showed the steepest early gain, rising from 0.10 at 5x to 0.47 at 25x and plateauing near 0.50 from 100x onward. DRUMMER exhibited a delayed onset, reaching 0.11 at 25x but climbing to 0.37 at 100x and 0.44 at 1000x. ELIGOS plateaued at ~0.27 by 50x with no further improvement at higher coverages.

Several tools with moderate-to-high AUROC achieved substantially lower AUPRC, for example, Tombo (AUROC 0.91, AUPRC 0.11) and EpiNano (AUROC 0.75, AUPRC 0.08) at 1000x on 23S, reflecting the sensitivity of AUPRC to false positives under class imbalance. Performance was broadly consistent across both rRNA references, though absolute AUPRC values were lower and more variable for 16S rRNA, likely due to its smaller number of true positives (11 vs 25).

## Nanocompore non-monotonic validity (low/mid/high coverage)

The middle-coverage Nanocompore issue is not primarily missing output files. The AUROC/AUPRC pipeline invalidates pvalue/FDR-based replicate metrics when invalid-score fraction exceeds 50%. For Nanocompore, invalid scores are dominated by `NaN` values in `GMM_logit_pvalue`. Across checked files, `NaN(GMM_logit_pvalue)` matched exactly the rows with `GMM_n_clust == 1` and `cluster_counts` containing `NC`. This explains the observed pattern: some low-coverage replicates pass (invalid fraction <=50%), many middle-coverages fail (invalid fraction >50%), and high coverages recover as the `NaN` fraction drops again.

## EpiNano mismatch vs sum_err in AUROC/AUPRC

Both EpiNano mismatch (`mis`) and sum-of-errors (`sum_err`) outputs were generated upstream. However, the downstream parser prioritises mismatch prediction files when both are present, so AUROC/AUPRC curves in this run were generated for a single EpiNano track (mismatch-derived z-score stream), not two separate EpiNano tracks.
